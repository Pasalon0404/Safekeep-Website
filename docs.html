Getting Started

Everything you need to go from cold hardware to your first signed transaction, start to finish.

Before you begin — read this

Always write your seed words and any passphrases down on paper and store them offline. Your 12- or 24-word mnemonic is the only unconditional backup of your Bitcoin. If you use a BIP-39 passphrase, that passphrase is equally critical — a seed without its passphrase cannot recover your funds. Write both down separately, store them in safe locations, and never store either digitally. No software, no hardware device, and no encrypted file can substitute for physical backups. If your vault drive fails, is lost, or is destroyed, your written seed words and passphrase are the only way to recover your funds. Do not skip this step. Write them down before you do anything else.

SafeKeepVault is currently in active development. While we take security seriously and the tools have been tested extensively, this software should be treated as a work in progress. We recommend starting with a small amount of funds while you get comfortable with the workflow, and always maintaining your written seed backup regardless of how much confidence you have in the software.

The Hardware Setup

SafeKeepVault enforces the air-gap in software. At boot, the OS disables all network drivers and the Bluetooth stack before any user code runs — the host machine's existing Wi-Fi card and ports are rendered inert without any physical modification. Your signing machine is a vault for the duration of the session.

You need three things:

  • The Boot Drive — A USB stick flashed with the SafeKeepVault image. This is your operating system. Plug it in, boot from it, and the machine becomes a dedicated signing device. The internal hard drive is never mounted.
  • A Dedicated Offline Machine — Any x86 laptop or desktop. It does not need to be expensive or powerful. SafeKeepVault disables all networking and Bluetooth at the kernel level during boot — no drivers are loaded, so the machine's existing hardware is irrelevant. No modifications to the host machine are required.
  • The Transfer Partition — The boot drive is partitioned to include an exFAT transfer volume alongside the OS. The Courier QR code optical transfer tool and the transfer drive are the only bridge between your vault and the outside world — unsigned transactions go in before boot, signed transactions come out after shutdown. USB ports are locked down once the OS is running, so no new devices are recognised mid-session; the drive must be moved between machines while the vault is powered off.

First Boot & Initialization

Insert the Boot Drive and power on. Your machine's BIOS should be configured to boot from USB. SafeKeepVault loads directly into the browser-based vault interface — no desktop, no terminal, no distractions.

One-Time Setup: Encrypted Storage

The very first time you boot, SafeKeepVault will walk you through a one-time setup step: creating your encrypted partition. This is the secure, persistent storage layer on the USB drive where your seed and vault data are kept between sessions. You will be asked to set a strong password to protect it.

Your password is your security

The strength of your encrypted drive password is the single most important security decision you will make in SafeKeepVault. The air-gap, the offline tools, the verified open-source code — none of it matters if your encrypted partition can be brute-forced. A weak password means anyone who gets physical access to your boot drive has access to your keys. There is no account to lock, no remote wipe, no second factor. The password is the lock. Make it unbreakable.

Use a password manager to generate a long, fully random password — at least 20 characters with mixed case, numbers, and symbols. Do not invent one yourself. Human-chosen passwords are predictable in ways that matter to an attacker even when they feel random to you. Let the machine generate it.

Once you have your password, use The Courier to encode it as a QR code and screenshot it to your phone. Store it in a locked photos album, your password manager's secure notes, or an encrypted vault app. On every subsequent boot, hold your phone up to the vault camera — the encrypted partition unlocks automatically without you ever typing a character. This is the recommended workflow: a machine-generated password stored in your password manager as the source of truth, with the QR code on your phone as the fast, camera-friendly unlock key. You can also type the password directly at boot if you prefer, but the QR method eliminates the temptation to use a shorter or simpler password because it is easier to type.

This setup step happens once. Every subsequent boot goes straight to the unlock prompt.

First Boot Options

Once your encrypted partition is unlocked, you'll see five options:

  • Forge a New Seed — launches The Forge tool, SafeKeepVault's entropy engine. Mix browser-generated entropy, dice rolls, and webcam noise into a single pool to generate a BIP-39 master mnemonic. You'll walk through a verification quiz to confirm your backup before the seed is written to the vault.
  • Restore an Existing Seed — enter your 12- or 24-word BIP-39 mnemonic to load a previously generated seed into the vault.
  • Seed XOR — load a seed by combining two or more XOR seed shares. Use this if your backup was split using The Shard tool XOR scheme. The shares are combined in memory and never written individually to storage.
  • Shamir Recovery — reconstruct a seed from SLIP-39 Shamir shares. Enter the required threshold of shares and The Covenant tool will recover the master secret and load it into the vault.
  • Restore from Backup — load a previously encrypted vault backup from your transfer drive. Use this to recover your full vault state, including any stored keys and configuration, onto a new or re-flashed boot drive.

Once your seed is loaded, you'll land on the Dashboard. The first thing you should do: click Mount Transfer Drive at the top of the screen. This grants the vault read/write access to the exFAT transfer partition on your boot drive. The transfer drive is how SafeKeepVault moves data in and out — use it to load unsigned PSBTs, save signed PSBTs, export PDF reports, and store encrypted backups. Because it is formatted as exFAT, you can mount it directly on any Mac or Windows machine to move files in and out between sessions.

If you prefer to stay fully air-gapped without ever plugging the drive into an online machine, use The Courier tool inside SafeKeepVault together with the live Courier tool on this website. The two communicate entirely via QR code — scan from your desktop browser or phone camera to transfer PSBTs and signed transactions back and forth across the air-gap without touching a shared drive.

You only need to mount the transfer drive once per session — every tool inherits the connection automatically. The status light in the Command Center bar will turn green when the drive is live.

The Tool Arsenal

Fifteen tools ship in the v1.1b image, organised in the dashboard under four working sections. Every tool is each one runs air-gapped, follows open Bitcoin standards, and produces output that any compatible wallet or coordinator can consume directly.

Transaction

  • The Scribe — Reviews, decodes, and signs Partially Signed Bitcoin Transactions entirely offline. Every input and output is displayed for inspection before the signature is committed across the air gap.
  • The Witness — BIP-322 message signing. Proves ownership of a Bitcoin address by signing an arbitrary message with the corresponding private key, producing a verifiable proof without moving any coins.

Seed Management

  • The Forge — Generates BIP-39 master seeds using a multi-source entropy engine. Mixes physical dice rolls, mouse movement, camera noise, and the kernel CSPRNG into a single pool — no single source is trusted on its own.
  • The Cipher — Generates, stores, and manages BIP-39 passphrases (the “25th word”) inside the encrypted vault. Entries sit alongside the seed and can be loaded directly into The Scribe when a PSBT requires a passphrase-locked wallet.
  • The Armory — Derives extended public keys, inspects derivation paths, and exports key material for watch-only wallet setup in Sparrow, Electrum, or any descriptor-aware coordinator. The master seed stays sealed — only public data leaves this screen.
  • The Script — Builds standardised output descriptors for single-sig and multisig configurations with full control over script type and derivation path. Use the exported descriptor to import a watch-only wallet into any compatible coordinator.
  • The Covenant — Splits the master seed into threshold shares using Shamir’s Secret Sharing (SLIP-39). Recovery requires only a quorum — any 2-of-3, 3-of-5, or similar scheme — and each individual share reveals nothing about the original.
  • The Shard — Splits the master seed into XOR shares for distributed cold storage, or merges existing shares to reconstruct the original. Unlike Shamir, every share is required for recovery — any subset reveals nothing.
  • The Lineage — Derives deterministic child seeds from the master seed using BIP-85. Lets you run isolated wallets for different purposes while maintaining only a single master backup — one seed to rule them all.

Backup & Verification

  • The Glyph — Encodes a seed as a compact SeedQR for stamping onto steel plates or printing on paper. Hand-drawn or engraved copies can be verified by scanning them back in with the built-in camera reader.
  • The Courier — Transfers PSBTs, descriptors, and other data between air-gapped devices using animated QR codes (BCR-UR / BBQr). No USB drives, no network — only light and a camera lens crossing the gap.
  • The Archive — A file browser for the Transfer Drive. Mount the exFAT partition to view, rename, or delete backup archives, signed transactions, and export files without ever leaving the air-gap.
  • The Reliquary — Exports selected vault contents as an AES-256 encrypted archive to the Transfer Drive. The 7z utility encrypts both file data and filenames, producing a sealed bundle that can only be opened with the correct password.

Workspace

  • The Codex — Stores plaintext notes inside the LUKS-encrypted vault. No secondary password is required — full-disk encryption protects everything at rest. Keep recovery instructions, multisig coordination details, and configuration notes sealed alongside your seed.
  • The Cartographer — Produces an authoritative technical map of your custody setup: exact derivation paths, account public keys, and script types an heir needs to reconstruct the wallet in Sparrow, Electrum, or any descriptor-aware coordinator. Print it and store it with your physical inheritance plan.

That’s the full life cycle: generate a seed, back it up across steel and encrypted archives, export your public keys, receive unsigned transactions, sign them in isolation, and carry the result back across the air-gap on a USB stick. No network. No clipboard. No attack surface.
